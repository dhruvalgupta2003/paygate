# Limen — Brand Story

## What the name means

**Limen** is the Latin word for *threshold* — the strip of stone or wood at the bottom of a doorway that separates one space from another. It's the root of the English words *liminal*, *subliminal*, *preliminary*, and *eliminate*.

A *limen* is the exact instant when something crosses from one state to another. The moment a sound becomes audible. The moment a request becomes authorized. The moment a free API call becomes a paid one.

That instant is what we sell.

## What we do

Every API request is a transition. Untrusted on one side. Authorized on the other. Limen sits at the threshold and decides whether the request crosses.

We're a paywall middleware for AI agent traffic. An agent makes a request. We respond `402 Payment Required` with a price in USDC. The agent signs a payment authorization. We verify it on-chain. We forward the request to your backend. The whole exchange takes about five seconds and costs about $0.0001 in gas.

No signup. No API keys. No invoicing. The protocol does the trust work — we just do the threshold.

## Why "Limen" and not "PayGate"

The original name pointed at the wrong half of the product.

A "gate" is a barrier. It implies blocking, restriction, friction. That's the *enemy* of what AI-agent commerce is supposed to feel like. Agents shouldn't experience your API as a wall they have to climb. They should experience it as a doorway they can walk through, provided they've paid the toll.

A *limen* is the doorway itself. Not the wall. Not the lock. The exact line where one domain ends and another begins. Standing at a limen is a neutral act — most agents will simply pay and pass through.

The shift in metaphor changes how we build:
- Gates emphasize *who is denied*. Limens emphasize *who can pass*.
- Gates are about defense. Limens are about commerce.
- Gates feel like security software. Limens feel like infrastructure.

We are infrastructure.

## The mark

A doorway standing on its threshold.

Two posts and a lintel describe the doorway — the architectural form a Roman builder would call a *trilithon*. Beneath the doorway, slightly wider and slightly heavier, runs a single horizontal line: the **limen** itself. In Latin, the lintel is *limen superum* and the threshold step is *limen inferum*. We draw the lower one explicitly. It is the foundation. It is the part of the structure that does the work.

```
   ┌───────────────┐         ← lintel (limen superum)
   │               │
   │               │         ← the doorway
   │               │
   │               │
 ─────────────────────       ← limen (limen inferum) — what we sell
```

The doorway is open at the bottom because passage is the point. An agent walks through it. The threshold underneath is the surface every transition crosses.

Three rules govern the mark:

1. **The doorway is open at the bottom.** A closed shape is a wall, not a threshold. Passage must be visible.
2. **The limen is wider and heavier than the doorway.** It extends past the posts on both sides and uses a thicker stroke. The foundation outweighs the structure.
3. **One ink color, square caps, mitered joins.** No gradients, no decorative fills, no rounded corners. The voice is architectural. Restraint is the brand.

## The voice

Confident. Brief. Architectural.

We sound like infrastructure that has already shipped many things and is no longer trying to prove itself.

- We say what works.
- We say what doesn't.
- We don't editorialize, exaggerate, or perform enthusiasm.
- We trust the reader to understand technical material.
- We avoid hype words: *revolutionary*, *seamless*, *cutting-edge*, *next-gen*.
- We avoid filler superlatives: *the best*, *the only*, *unparalleled*.

If a sentence reads like marketing, we cut it.

## What this brand is not

- We are not crypto-aesthetic. No glow effects. No neon. No gradients to "purple money."
- We are not Stripe. We don't try to be cute about being un-crypto.
- We are not a SaaS dashboard template with our logo dropped in. The mark is intentional. The palette is intentional. The negative space is intentional.
- We are not pretending to be larger than we are. The README says exactly what works and what doesn't, with file paths.
